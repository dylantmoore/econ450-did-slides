The Selection Problem

Naive comparison:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y \mid D=1] \;-\; \mathbb{E}[Y \mid D=0]$$

$$\mathbb{E}[Y|D\!=\!1] - \mathbb{E}[Y|D\!=\!0] \;=\; \underbrace{\color{#0072B2}{\text{ATT}}}_{\text{Causal effect}} \;+\; \underbrace{\color{#D55E00}{\mathbb{E}[Y(0)|D\!=\!1] - \mathbb{E}[Y(0)|D\!=\!0]}}_{\color{#D55E00}{\text{Selection bias}}}$$

Selection bias arises because treated and control groups may differ even without treatment. People who choose treatment are often systematically different.

Lecture Notes: Selection Bias in Practice

Selection bias is what makes the naive comparison (treated vs. control outcomes) fail. Even without treatment, these groups may have had different outcomes.

Returns to education: If I compare people who went to college with people who did not, their earnings would have differed for many reasons besides college itself. People who attend college tend to have higher ability, more motivated families, better high school preparation, etc. The earnings gap includes both the causal effect of college and all these pre-existing differences.

In the real world, treatment assignment often reflects political calculation and strategic decisions, not randomization. People lobby for policies that benefit their constituents. Special interest groups push for favorable treatment. So it is rarely random who ends up being treated by a policy.

This is exactly why we need a strategy—like DID—to separate the causal effect from these pre-existing differences.
