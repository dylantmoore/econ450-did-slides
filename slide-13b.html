Lecture Notes: Boning, Hendren, Sprung-Keyser & Stuart (QJE, 2025)

Citation: “A Welfare Analysis of Tax Audits Across the Income Distribution,” Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 140, Issue 1, pp. 63–142.

The left figure shows what happens when you study all IRS audits. Operational audits are not randomly assigned—the IRS targets people who look suspicious. As a result, audited taxpayers had recently experienced an income bump (which likely triggered the audit). This creates a clear pre-trend violation: the treatment group’s income was already diverging from the control group before the audit occurred.

The right figure uses only the National Research Program (NRP) audits, which are randomly assigned. The NRP selects a stratified random sample of returns for review to estimate the “tax gap.” With random assignment, there is no reason for treatment and control groups to differ before the audit. Sure enough, the pre-trends are flat near zero.

Key finding: Audited taxpayers pay about $300 more per year in additional taxes, and this effect persists for at least 14 years with no visible decay. In present value, these deterrence effects raise 3.2× as much revenue as the initial audit itself. Returns to audits rise sharply with income: every $1 spent auditing taxpayers above the 90th percentile yields about $12 in revenue.

Why this matters for DID: The non-random audits would give a misleading picture because selection into treatment is correlated with the outcome trajectory. An earlier study (DeBacker et al., 2018) used NRP data but omitted year-since-audit fixed effects and found a negative deterrence effect for high-income taxpayers—that result disappears with the correct specification. This illustrates how specification choices in event studies can matter enormously.
