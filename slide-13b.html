Case Study: IRS Audits & Tax Payments

Do audits increase future tax payments? Comparing non-random vs. randomized audits shows why parallel trends matter.

Non-Random Audits

Pre-trends are violated: audited taxpayers had a bump in income before being selected for audit.

Randomized (NRP) Audits

NRP audits are randomly assigned: pre-trends are flat and effects are credibly causal.

Lecture Notes: Why Two Figures?

The left figure shows what happens when you study all IRS audits. These audits are not randomly assigned—the IRS targets people who look suspicious. As a result, the audited taxpayers had recently experienced an income bump (which may have triggered the audit). This creates a clear pre-trend violation: the treatment group's income was already declining relative to the control group before the audit.

The right figure uses only the National Research Program (NRP) audits, which are randomly assigned. With random assignment, there is no reason for the treatment and control groups to differ before the audit. Sure enough, the pre-trends are flat near zero.

The NRP results show that audits do increase long-run tax payments—audited taxpayers pay about $300 more per year for over a decade. This is likely because the audit uncovered previously unreported income, and taxpayers now know the IRS is aware of it.

This is a vivid illustration of why pre-trends matter: the non-random audits would give a misleading picture because selection into treatment is correlated with the outcome trajectory. The randomized audits give us the clean answer.
