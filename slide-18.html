Pre-Trends as a Test

Bad Pre-Trends

Pre-period effects trending upward → parallel trends likely violated

Good Pre-Trends

Pre-period effects near zero → parallel trends supported

If pre-treatment coefficients are significantly different from zero or show a trend, the parallel trends assumption is suspect.

Lecture Notes: What to Do When Pre-Trends Are Imperfect

Bad pre-trends do not always kill your analysis. They do mean you need to think much more carefully about what is going on.

Consider a case where the pre-treatment coefficients show a downward trend (the treatment group was falling relative to the control). If, after treatment, that trend suddenly reverses—now the treatment group starts gaining—that change in trajectory might itself be evidence of a causal effect.

Think of it this way: the simplest version of the parallel trends assumption says the difference between the two groups should be flat. But if it was trending downward before treatment and then suddenly trends upward, that reversal coinciding exactly with treatment is still a kind of "surprise." It is a more complicated story, and it requires more sophisticated methods to estimate the magnitude of the effect, but it is not necessarily a deal-breaker.

The bad pre-trend story that does kill your analysis is the one on the left: an upward trend that just continues through the treatment period with no visible break. In that case, there is no surprise, and the post-treatment estimates are just a continuation of what was already happening.
