DID as a 2×2 Table

Pre (t=0) Post (t=1) Difference
Treatment 10.0 15.6 +5.6
Control 10.0 11.7 +1.7
DID =5.6 − 1.7 = 3.9

The DID is literally the difference of the two differences!

Lecture Notes: Working Through the Numbers

The DID calculation is straightforward: the treatment group went from 10.0 to 15.6 (a change of +5.6). The control group went from 10.0 to 11.7 (a change of +1.7). The second difference is 5.6 − 1.7 = 3.9.

What does the 1.7 represent? It is what happened to the control group—and under the parallel trends assumption, it is what would have happened to the treatment group absent the policy. So we expected 11.7 for the treatment group, but we observed 15.6. The difference of 3.9 is our estimate of the causal effect.

On the exam: You will be given numbers and asked to compute the DID estimate. The arithmetic is the easy part. The harder (and more important) part will be: based on evidence presented, do you think the parallel trends assumption is reasonable? If not, what are the implications?
