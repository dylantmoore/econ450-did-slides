“No Causes In, No Causes Out”

— Nancy Cartwright, philosopher of science

There is no such thing as purely “data-driven” causal inference. Every causal claim combines data with assumptions that cannot themselves be tested with data alone.

In DID, that assumption is parallel trends. So how do we build confidence in it?

Lecture Notes: Cartwright's Principle

Nancy Cartwright is a philosopher of science who has written extensively about causal reasoning. Her phrase “no causes in, no causes out” captures a deep point: you cannot extract causal knowledge from a statistical procedure unless you feed causal assumptions into that procedure.

This applies to every causal method in econometrics—DID, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity, randomized experiments. Even randomized experiments require the assumption that randomization was implemented correctly and that there is no attrition or noncompliance that violates the design.

Why this matters for DID: Students sometimes think that if the pre-trends look parallel, they have “proven” that the design works. But parallel pre-trends are evidence in favor of the assumption, not proof. The assumption is about what would have happened—a counterfactual—and no amount of data can directly verify a counterfactual.

The right mindset is: causal inference is always a combination of data and judgment. Your job is to make the strongest case you can, knowing that certainty is not available.
