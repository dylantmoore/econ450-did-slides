The Event Study Regression

$$Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{\substack{k \neq -1}} \color{#0072B2}{\beta_k} \cdot D_i \cdot \mathbf{1}\!\left(t - t^*_i = k\right) + u_{it}$$

\(\alpha_i\) = unit fixed effects (absorb time-invariant differences between groups)
\(\gamma_t\) = time fixed effects (absorb common shocks affecting all units)
\(D_i\) = treatment indicator (1 if unit i is ever treated)
\(\mathbf{1}(t - t^*_i = k)\) = indicator for being \(k\) periods from treatment
\(\beta_k\) = the treatment effect at relative time \(k\) (what we plot!)
\(k \neq -1\) we exclude one period as the reference (normalization)

Lecture Notes: Don't Get Lost in the Equation

This regression equation is just the formal version of what we already described graphically. You do not need to memorize the math—the important thing is to understand what the pieces mean intuitively.

The unit fixed effects (\(\alpha_i\)) handle the fact that treatment and control groups may have different baseline levels. The time fixed effects (\(\gamma_t\)) handle the fact that things change over time for everyone. What is left over—the \(\beta_k\) coefficients—capture how the treatment group deviates from the parallel trends prediction at each point in time.

Why exclude \(k = -1\)? We need a reference point. By omitting the period just before treatment, we force the event study plot to pass through zero at \(t = -1\). Everything else is measured relative to that moment. It is like setting a ruler's zero mark—you need to anchor the scale somewhere.

In this class, we will focus more on the graphical intuition than on the regression mechanics. If you understand the event study plot, you understand what this regression is doing.
