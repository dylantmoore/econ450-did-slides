Lecture Notes: Levels vs. Percentage Changes

When the treatment and control groups start from different levels, there is an important subtlety: "parallel trends" in levels is not the same as "parallel trends" in percentage changes.

Example: Suppose the control group starts at 10 and goes to 20 (an increase of 10, or 100%). The treatment group starts at 20. Under parallel trends in levels, we expect the treatment group to also increase by 10 (to 30). Under parallel trends in percentages, we expect the treatment group to also increase by 100% (to 40). These give very different counterfactuals.

When both groups start at the same level, the two definitions coincide—going up by 1.7 and going up by 17% give the same answer. But once the baselines differ, you get different answers depending on which assumption you make.

In practice, economists often default to assuming the percentage change version is more appropriate (which corresponds to taking logs of the outcome). But there is nothing automatic about this choice—you need context-specific reasoning about which assumption is more plausible.
