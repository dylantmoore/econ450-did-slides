Average Treatment Effects

Average Treatment Effect (ATE)

$$\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}\!\big[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)\big]$$

The average effect across the entire population

Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT)

$$\text{ATT} = \mathbb{E}\!\big[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \;\big|\; D_i = 1\big]$$

The average effect for those who actually received treatment

DID estimates the ATT — the effect for the treated group

Lecture Notes: ATE vs. ATT

The \(\mathbb{E}[\cdot]\) notation just means "average." Don't be intimidated by it—just think of it as asking: what is the average value of the thing inside?

In a randomized control trial, ATE and ATT are the same thing. Because treatment was assigned randomly, there is no meaningful distinction between the people who got treated and the general population.

In a DID design, we can only estimate the ATT. Here is why: we build a counterfactual for the treatment group using the control group's trajectory. That tells us what would have happened to the treated group absent treatment. But we learn nothing about what the treatment effect would have been for the control group had they been treated.

To claim ATE = ATT, you would need to assume the treatment effect is the same for both groups. But there is no pre-treatment evidence that could support this—it is a fundamentally different kind of assumption from parallel trends.
