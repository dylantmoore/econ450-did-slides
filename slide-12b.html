Building Confidence in Your Results

Parallel trends can never be proven. But we can accumulate evidence that makes it more or less credible.

Placebo Tests

A placebo test asks: if I apply my method to a situation where I know nothing happened, do I correctly find zero?

Grocery store example: The researchers applied their DID method to the control stores—where no price change occurred—and showed that the estimated effect was zero. This is a placebo test: if the method works where nothing happened, we have more confidence it works where something did happen.

Context & Qualitative Evidence

Pre-trends are not the only evidence that matters. Researchers also build a qualitative case for why parallel trends should hold.

This might include:

  • The treatment and control units are geographically close
  • They serve similar populations or markets
  • Treatment assignment was somewhat arbitrary or quasi-random
  • There is no obvious reason for differential trends
Grocery store example: The stores were in the same neighborhood in California, sold the same products, and had similar levels of demand. The researchers argued there was no particular reason one store was chosen over the other two. This institutional context is what really supports the assumption—not just the graph.

Bouncing Post-Treatment Effects

When the post-treatment coefficients bounce around (positive one period, negative the next), it is hard to tell a coherent causal story.

This usually means one of two things:

  • No real effect: There is genuinely nothing happening, and the estimates are just noise.
  • Insufficient power: Your sample is too small to detect an effect, even if one exists. The wide confidence intervals are the telltale sign.

Change in Trajectory

Bad pre-trends do not always kill your analysis. What matters is whether there is a visible break at the moment of treatment.

If the treatment group was trending downward relative to the control before treatment, and then suddenly reverses to trend upward, that reversal coinciding with treatment is still a kind of "surprise." It may still indicate a causal effect, but estimating the magnitude requires more sophisticated methods than simple DID.

The story that truly kills DID is one where the post-treatment pattern is just a smooth continuation of the pre-treatment trend—no break, no surprise.
